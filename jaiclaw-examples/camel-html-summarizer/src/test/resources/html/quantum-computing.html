Quantum Computing Reaches a Turning Point

After decades of theoretical research, quantum computing is entering a practical era. Companies like IBM, Google, and startups such as IonQ are building machines with hundreds of qubits, moving beyond proof-of-concept demonstrations toward solving real optimization problems in logistics, drug discovery, and financial modeling. Error correction remains the biggest hurdle — current machines are noisy and require thousands of physical qubits to create a single reliable logical qubit. Yet hybrid quantum-classical algorithms are already showing promise, letting today's imperfect hardware tackle problems that would take classical supercomputers years to solve. The race is now about who can scale first.
